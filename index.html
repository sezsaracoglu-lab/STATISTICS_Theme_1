The Big Picture
The single core question: "How do we know what we know?" — and why science answers it better than anything else.
🧠 The Master Narrative
Humans start with tenacity ("always believed") and authority ("experts said so") — fast but unreliable. We upgrade to reason (logic) and empiricism (observation), but each fails alone. Science solves this with Systematic Empiricism: logic + structured observation working together. Scientists build theories, test hypotheses, chase causal explanations, and use control to eliminate alternative explanations.

Ways of Acquiring Knowledge
Four non-scientific methods + the scientific synthesis. The Three-Door Problem is the gateway case study.
🚪 The Monty Hall Problem — Your Anchor Story
3 doors: 1 car, 2 goats. You pick Door 1. Host opens a goat door. Do you switch?

Intuition says: 50/50 — doesn't matter. (Tenacity at work)
Reason says: ALWAYS switch. Your door = 1/3. The other group = 2/3. Host concentrates that 2/3 onto one door.

This controversy — where academics attacked Marilyn vos Savant's correct answer — is the perfect illustration of Tenacity overriding Reason.

The 4 Non-Scientific Ways

🧱 Tenacity

What: Believing because you always have. Force of habit.
Strength: Psychologically comfortable, quick decisions.
Fatal flaw: Zero self-correction. Stubbornly resists counter-evidence.
Example: "Staying" with Door 1 because your gut said so."

📚 Authority

What: Accepting facts from trusted/respected sources — parents, experts, textbooks.
Strength: Efficient — essential in specialist societies.
Fatal flaw: Truth is entirely dependent on the source's credibility, expertise, and objectivity. Uncritical acceptance = danger.
The misconception: Authority is NOT always bad — uncritical authority is.

🔍 Reason (A Priori)

What: Logic and intellectual rationality. Deductive/inductive arguments (syllogisms).
Strength: If premises are true → conclusions must be true (deductively).
Fatal flaw: The Problem of Premises. A conclusion can be logically valid but empirically false if the premise is wrong.
Example: "Infants understand Object Permanence only after 9 months" — Piaget's logical framework, empirically overturned.

👁️ Empiricism

What: Knowledge from direct observation/experience using the senses.
Strength: Grounds claims in objective, external reality.
Fatal flaw: Unaided empiricism fails because of:
  ① Confirmation Bias — only noticing confirming info
  ② Lack of Controls — can't rule out confounds
  ③ Failure to consider PAEs — reverse causality goes unnoticed

The Scientific Solution — Systematic Empiricism

⚗️ Systematic Empiricism

The synthesis that fixes both Reason's and Empiricism's blind spots. Three steps:

1. Collect data in a structured, controlled way (controlled experiments)
2. Evaluate data with rigorous rational logic (Reason/Statistics) against a hypothesis
3. Develop, test, refine explanations (hypotheses → theories) based on the data

Reason provides the logical framework (testable hypotheses). Systematic Empiricism provides the rigorous, objective data to test and correct them.
Psychology is scientific ONLY because it adheres to this methodology.

⚡ Key Distinction: Sunk Cost Fallacy
Tenacity in disguise. Staying with Door 1 because "I already chose it" — an investor stubbornly keeping a bad stock because they've invested too much to quit. Irrational adherence to a poor choice to avoid feeling the loss of an initial investment.

🔎 Description — "What?"

Identifying and portraying the characteristics, phenomena, or facts of a behavior. This includes defining, classifying, and measuring variables.

Example: Describing the prevalence and demographics of binge drinking among college students.

Basic research typically operates at this level.

💡 Explanation — "Why?"

Determining the causes of a phenomenon. This is the most intellectually demanding goal. Requires meeting 3 necessary conditions:

① Temporal Order — cause precedes effect
② Covariation — cause and effect co-vary
③ Absence of PAEs — all confounds ruled out

Requires controlled experimental design.

📡 Prediction — "When?"

Forecasting future outcomes based on current understanding.

Critical distinction: Prediction ≠ Explanation. A variable can be a good predictor (high correlation) without being the true cause.

Example: High School GPA predicts College GPA well — but motivation may be the true underlying cause of both.

🎛️ Control — "How to influence?"

The final, ultimate goal of science. Works in two ways:

1. Experimental Control (Method): Manipulate IV, hold all other variables constant. Rules out PAEs.

2. Applied Control (Goal): Apply scientific knowledge to modify a phenomenon in a desired direction — e.g., developing behavioral therapy programs.

Hierarchy: Description ≥ Explanation ≥ Prediction → Control

⚠️ Exam Trap — "Prediction Equals Explanation": A classic mistake. Because you can predict X from Y (high correlation), X must cause Y. FALSE. A third variable Z could cause both. Correlation is necessary but never sufficient for causation.
Causal Inference & PAEs
The hardest part of science. Three criteria, two classic traps, one solution.

The 3 Criteria for Causation

2
📈
Covariation

The Causal Chain — Distal vs Proximal

Complex psychological phenomena rarely have a single cause — they emerge from a chain of linked causes over time.

🧬
Distal Cause — Remote/Early-Stage
Genetic Vulnerability / Prenatal Insults
Remote, early-stage factors that increase vulnerability but do not immediately trigger the outcome. Examples in schizophrenia: inherited genetic mutations, prenatal infections.
🌿
Distal Cause — Developmental
Early Childhood Adversity / Trauma
Accumulated vulnerability factors during development. Still remote — building the causal chain toward eventual outcome.
⚡
Proximal Cause — Immediate/Triggering
Stressful Life Events / Drug Use in Young Adulthood
Immediate, close-in-time factors that directly trigger or contribute to the outcome, especially in already vulnerable individuals. This is the proximate cause — the trigger that fires the loaded gun.
💥
Outcome
Psychological Disorder / Behavior
The result of the full causal chain. Understanding the whole chain — not just the trigger — is essential for correct explanation and effective intervention.

The 2 Types of PAEs (Plausible Alternative Explanations)

🔄 PAE Type 1: Reverse Causality

The assumed effect is actually the cause.

Example: We observe Team Cohesion → Winning. But what if Winning → Team Cohesion? The experience of success is what creates the bond.

This is a challenge to the Temporal Order criterion — the causal direction is ambiguous.

🔀 PAE Type 2: Third-Variable Problem

A third unmeasured variable (Z) causes changes in both X and Y, making their correlation entirely spurious.

Example: Superior Talent (Z) → both High Cohesion (X) AND Winning (Y). The cohesion-winning correlation is a coincidence driven by Z.

This is the challenge to the Absence of PAEs criterion.

The Solution — Experimental Control

🧪 Methodological Control

The only way to systematically rule out PAEs. Two primary techniques:

Double-Blind Design: Controls for psychological biases — especially the Placebo Effect. Neither participants nor researchers know who received the treatment. Controls both participant bias and experimenter bias.

Random Assignment: Randomly assigns participants to conditions. Controls for unknown third variables (e.g., talent, genetics) by distributing them equally across groups by chance.

The Gold Standard — Balanced Placebo Design

Separates the effect of the actual substance from the effect of the belief in the substance (Placebo Effect).

Characteristics of Science
What makes science self-correcting — and why it has real limitations.
📐
Empirical & Systematic
Relies on Systematic Empiricism — structured observation + reason/logic to test and evaluate claims.
❌
Falsifiability (Popper)
A theory must be capable of being proven false by empirical observation. The fence between science and non-science.
🎯
Accuracy & Objectivity
Knowledge must reliably reflect what's observed, uninfluenced by personal beliefs or emotions. Achieved via Operational Definitions and double-blind designs.
📝
Operational Definitions
Concepts defined in terms of specific procedures used to measure or manipulate them. Essential for replication.
📢
Public Reporting
Findings published via peer-reviewed journals so they can be independently verified, criticized, and replicated.
🔁
Replication
The ultimate test of a finding's reliability. Conducting a study again with same procedures. Key to science's self-correction.

The Falsifiability Principle — Karl Popper

✅ Falsifiable (Scientific)

"Exposure to loud noise increases cortisol levels" — This can be tested. You could measure cortisol after quiet vs loud exposure. A result of no-change would falsify it.

"Aggressive behavior in children increases with TV violence exposure" — Measurable, testable, falsifiable.

❌ Non-Falsifiable (Non-Scientific)

Metaphysical claims about faith, ultimate purpose, or morality — no conceivable empirical observation could disprove them.

Claims that can explain any possible outcome are non-falsifiable — and therefore unscientific.

Limitations of Science

⚖️ What Science Cannot Do

No absolute proof: All scientific conclusions are probabilistic — science provides the best available explanation at a given time, always open to revision.

Cannot answer non-empirical questions: Questions of faith, ultimate purpose, ethics, and morality are outside science's domain.

Knowledge is tentative: Scientific knowledge is provisional — not permanent truth. New replicated data can falsify (disprove) older findings.

🔬 Operational Definitions — Deep Dive
Why they matter: "Aggression" means nothing until you define it as "number of times a child pushes another child during a 30-minute play session." Now it's:
• Observable and measurable
• Replicable by any researcher
• Objective (removes personal interpretation)

Exam application: You will be given a vague construct (love, happiness, intelligence) and asked to write a rigorous Operational Definition for it.

🔄 Interdependence — The Crucial Point
Basic research provides the fundamental theories → Applied research uses those principles to solve problems → Applied research, in turn, raises new questions that feed back into Basic research. The two are highly interdependent and reciprocal. Neither is superior.

Anecdotal Evidence — Why It Fails

📖 Anecdotal Evidence

Brief reports or descriptions of personal experiences or specific instances used to support a claim.

Why it is scientifically weak:
It is a form of unaided empiricism and fails to control for the three critical PAEs:
  ① Spontaneous Remission — the condition improved on its own
  ② Placebo Effect — improvement due to expectation alone
  ③ Confounding Variables — a third factor caused the improvement

⚠️ Most Common Misconception: "Personal experience is good evidence because it's direct observation." WRONG. Anecdotal evidence is unaided empiricism with zero control. The 3 classic PAEs are almost never ruled out.
Misconception Flashcards
Click each card to flip between the misconception and the correction.
❌ Misconception

"Authority is always bad and unnecessary in science — we should never trust experts."

✅ Correction

Authority is efficient and often necessary (e.g., trust a surgeon for medical advice). The problem is uncritical acceptance. Science requires checking credentials, objectivity, and empirical basis.

❌ Misconception

"Prediction equals Explanation. If I can predict X from Y, then Y must cause X."

✅ Correction

FALSE. High correlation/covariation only satisfies criterion 2 of causation. A third variable Z could cause both, or the direction could be reversed. Correlation is necessary but never sufficient for causation.

❌ Misconception

"Scientific theories are just guesses — they're uncertain and unproven."

✅ Correction

In science, a theory is a systematic body of ideas repeatedly tested and strongly supported by empirical evidence (e.g., Evolutionary Theory, Germ Theory). It is emphatically NOT a guess.

❌ Misconception

"Anecdotal evidence is good evidence because it's based on direct observation."

✅ Correction

Anecdotal evidence is scientifically weak because it is unaided empiricism and fails to control for Spontaneous Remission, the Placebo Effect, and Confounding Variables.

❌ Misconception

"Skepticism means being cynical — never believing anything."

✅ Correction

Skepticism is an intellectual posture of questioning claims and demanding empirical evidence. It is open-minded and evidence-driven. Cynicism is closed-minded dismissal. They are opposites.

❌ Misconception

"The Monty Hall problem is 50/50 after the host opens a door — it resets."

✅ Correction

The probability does NOT reset. Your original door = 1/3. The host's non-random action concentrates the 2/3 group probability onto the remaining door. Always switch: 2/3 vs 1/3.

Exam Pattern Analysis

🎯 High-Probability Essay — Three-Door Problem
You will NOT just be asked the answer ("switch"). You'll be asked to explain WHY the probability changes — i.e., that the host's action is non-random and concentrates the original 2/3 probability onto the remaining door. The ability to reconstruct and explain the logic table is essential.
🎯 High-Probability Application — Causation/PAEs
Expect a scenario (like Team Cohesion → Winning). You must: 1) state the 3 criteria for causal explanation; 2) identify a specific Reverse Causality problem (Y → X); 3) identify a specific Third-Variable Problem (Z → X and Y); 4) explain what specific control method would rule out those PAEs.
🎯 High-Probability Definition — Operational Definition / Falsifiability
You'll be given a vague psychological construct (e.g., "love," "happiness," "intelligence") and asked to develop an appropriate, rigorous Operational Definition. Separately: given a claim, state whether it is or is not falsifiable (Popper) and justify why.
